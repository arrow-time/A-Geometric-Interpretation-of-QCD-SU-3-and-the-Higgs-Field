1. Following Einstein's formulation of General Relativity (GR), gravitational theory entered the geometric era, with this geometric framework of gravity becoming the mainstream in academia. In the 1960s, Wheeler proposed the concept of "mass without mass," suggesting that mass itself is geometry. This provides an intuitive image where mass is a condensation of space, inducing a geometric stretching of the surrounding space. This compelling and attractive image offers a specific mechanism for how mass generates gravity.

2. Subsequently, the scientific community engaged in extensive and multifaceted discussions on unifying quantum field theories, yielding numerous outcomes. The primary path involves attempts to combine the geometry of GR with the gauge symmetry of the Standard Model of Quantum Field Theory (QFT) to construct a space possessing both geometric and quantized configurations. Since the mid-20th century, theories such as soliton theory, quantized elastic analog models, loop quantum gravity, analog gravity, modified gravity, entropic gravity, string theory, and holographic duality have emerged. In essence, various theories, from realist or purely mathematical instrumentalist perspectives, have attempted to fuse the geometry of relativity with the quantum mechanisms of QFT to construct a unified field. In the language of QFT, this naturally leads to the hypothesis of a fundamental quantum field from which all microscopic particles are excitations.

3. The Problem: The geometric degrees of freedom are incompatible with the gauge degrees of freedom. GR's geometric degrees of freedom are based on diffeomorphism invariance, whereas the Standard Model of QFT, which explains electromagnetic, weak, and strong interactions (QCD) and the Higgs mechanism, relies on degrees of freedom like spin. The gauge group's degrees of freedom inevitably disrupt the differential topological homeomorphism of GR at the Planck scale. This contradiction represents the most significant ontological obstacle to a unified field theory. Previous research has attempted to either abandon GR's diffeomorphism invariance, introducing dynamic reconnection of geometric degrees of freedom (like topology), or, as in string theory, bypass ontological realism altogether, seeking solutions directly from mathematical constructions.

It proposes a quantized elastic two-layer base space model.
